Grande Sonata Pathétique

Sonata No. 8; Op. 13

Introduction

The Sonata No. 8, Op. 13 was composed by Beethoven in 1798 and was published in the year 1799. It was dedicated to Prince Karl von Lichnowsky, a good friend and artistic patron of Beethoven. It is unknown whether it was Beethoven or his publishers that gave it the nickname "Pathétique" (meaning full of emotion or sorrow or "pathos") but it is known that Beethoven found the name acceptable, at the very least.

Even in Beethoven's day, this was a very famous and popular sonata: James (2016) states that it was so popular that arrangements for wind nonet, string quartet and quintet, piano quartet, theatre orchestra, and other such musical ensembles have been made throughout the years. It captures emotion in a way that few pieces thence did, and seriously propelled the musical zeitgeist forwards from the Classical Era into the Early Romantic Era.

Much as previous sonatas were, the first movement is the most prominent, being in sonata form and taking the most time of all the movements. It also contains much of the pathos or emotion in the whole movement, and is in the key of C minor or 3 flats. The second movement is the slow movement in Adagio Cantabile, or "slowly and in a singing style": it is in the key of A-flat major, with 4 flats. Lastly, the third movement is a Rondo, and is in the home key of C minor. I believe this sonata both exemplifies our understanding of traditional, classical, sonatas, while also displaying Beethoven's style of writing and thinking.
