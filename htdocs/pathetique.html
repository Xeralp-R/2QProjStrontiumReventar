Introduction

The Sonata No. 8, Op. 13 was composed by Beethoven in 1798 and was published in the year 1799. It was dedicated to Prince Karl von Lichnowsky, a good friend and artistic patron of Beethoven. It is unknown whether it was Beethoven or his publishers that gave it the nickname "Pathétique" (meaning full of emotion or sorrow or "pathos") but it is known that Beethoven found the name acceptable, at the very least.

Even in Beethoven's day, this was a very famous and popular sonata: James (2016) states that it was so popular that arrangements for wind nonet, string quartet and quintet, piano quartet, theatre orchestra, and other such musical ensembles have been made throughout the years. It captures emotion in a way that few pieces thence did, and seriously propelled the musical zeitgeist forwards from the Classical Era into the Early Romantic Era.

Much as previous sonatas were, the first movement is the most prominent, being in sonata form and taking the most time of all the movements. It also contains much of the pathos or emotion in the whole movement, and is in the key of C minor or 3 flats. The second movement, not discussed for the sake of brevity, is the slow movement in Adagio Cantabile, or "slowly and in a singing style": it is in the key of A-flat major, with 4 flats. Lastly, the third movement, also not discussed, is a Rondo, and is in the home key of C minor. I believe this sonata both exemplifies our understanding of traditional, classical, sonatas, while also displaying Beethoven's style of writing and thinking.

The first movement is quite long compared to most sonatas of the time, clocking in at 310 measures or around 8 pages in most editions. As with most other sonatas of the time, this first movement is in a definite sonata form, albeit we already begin to see the introduction and the coda that are usually not so much a part of the textbook form.

The score was taken from the Wallner Edition, which was published by Henle Verlag in 1976. That edition is an urtext edition, so while I believe it is legal here in the Philippines, I am not so sure about the situations in other countries. The same applies for the recordings which I have duly cut up into the component parts: These were made by Fritz Jank in 1962. All of these resources can be found in the International Music Score Library Project, ot the Petrucci Music Library.

Sonata Introduction

Introductory Theme

The sonata begins with a huge C-minor chord, with all hands playing some seven notes: even from the beginning, the piece evokes grandeur and sorrow, like few others before. James states that this chord would have shaken many fortepianos to their cores in Beethoven's day with their sheer weight. After this, the melody suddenly softens and goes up 2 steps, repeats the uppermost note, and then goes down 1 step. This rising-falling motif is important: it reappears later on, both in the introduction and also in the development, some 100 bars away. The constant recurrence of this motif is the reason why Andras Schiff states that this part is no mere introduction, but instead constitutes the first theme in the exposition: whether you agree or disagree with this view is up to you, but it will have effects later on.

Now, before we go on to the rest of the bars, we should take note of 2 things. First, the tempo is not Allegro, as was common with most first movements of the time, but instead is Grave. These tempi lie on the opposite ends of the spectrum of speeds: while Allegro means "lively" upon being translated from Italian, and is related to words like "alacrity", you don't have to know Italian to understand that Grave means playing gravely, slowly, and solemnly. This tempo difference, especially compared to the material 10 measures on, is why most consider this part just to be an introduction: for example, another introduction like this lies with the Les Adieux Sonata, which I discuss elsewhere. Second, the dynamic marking is highly unusual: fp, or forte-piano, means to play loudly and then suddenly softly. However, this effect is impossible on the piano: a note will decay for however long you hold the note and will disappear upon releasing the note, meaning that there is no way to play the same note softly. Of course, skilled players like Schiff have found a workaround, by repeating the note softly while releasing the pedal holding the loud note (albeit I am not entirely sure if this is correct, because I unfortunately did not see the lecture with my own eyes)– but, even so, it is somewhat unusual and unpianistic. This often leads people to say it feels more like a piano reduction of a symphony or a reduction of a piano concerto rather than a piano sonata— which may be the reason why it has been arranged for other instruments so many times.

Moving on, we see that the forte-piano chords and the rising-falling motif are repeated in the second measure, but this time moved up— the same occurs with the third bar, with the motif repeated twice. Each time the forte-piano chord recurs, it becomes more and more dissonant, liberally using tritones to evoke the gut-wrenching characteristic of this part, until it reaches the end of 4th measure and calms slightly with a scalar passage reminiscent of the Fantasias of Bach.

Expansion of Introductory Theme

We now move on to the 4th to 10th measures. We can view it as something of a drama between 2 characters: the first has a pleading, softer voice, while the second has a voice of refusal, anger, and strength. This is reflected by the usage of different registers and dynamics of the piano. The angrier voice has a range somewhere between middle C and high C, which is close to the range of a tenor, albeit the lowest notes approach the bass range. It also always plays at a fortissimo dynamic, which means to play very loudly. On the other hand, the pleading voice has a range from high C to the C the octave above that, which approximates the range of a soprano. It also plays piano, or softly. So I think this is a fair representation of that.

With regards to the music, both use the same melodic idea, the rising-falling motif: this idea occurs both in the higher register and in the lower register, but slightly altered and with the harmonies filled in. On the left hand, we have a chromatically descending bass: while the bass is usually punctuated by the fortissimo chords, which obscure the descent somewhat, it is very clear from bars 7 to 8, when it constantly descends as it is counterbalanced by the ascending right hand until the melody peaks at bar 9.

In bars 9 and 10, the music thins significantly, with many dramatic pauses: the left hand comes down octave by octave and pulls the right hand down with it. In the second half of measure 10, we get a soft chromatic scale running down 2 octaves (this scale is not supposed to be showy nor virtuosic, but rather deflationary, according to James) until we reach the A flat, and proceed to resolve to the Allegro. Note that the transition between the 2 parts is sudden, without pause: contrast is one of the hallmarks of a Beethoven Sonata, and here the contrast would be between tempos, as elsewhere it may be with the dynamics.

Exposition

Theme 1.1

The allegro begins with the first theme, what is referred to as a "Mannheim Rocket": a huge, ascending, arpeggio continuously going upwards and upwards until it reaches its peak 2 octaves later. This technique features prominently in the Mannheim school of composition: a notable example would be with the finale of Mozart's 40th Symphony. However, while the natural tendency would be to crescendo over the upward run, Beethoven instructs the performer to play softly– this evokes the feeling of tension rather than a violent outburst, albeit those will invariably come later. After running up for some 4 bars, it begins to sink back down, loudening more and more until the repetition of the phrase. This 8-bar phrase composes the first theme of the sonata, and we will be playing around with it more a little bit later. For convenience, I will call this Theme 1.1.

Now, I think it's somewhat important for me to mention the left hand: it is playing notes with a tremolo effect, rapidly moving from one note to the other. This is yet another unpianistic effect in this sonata: tremolos are very easy to do on the violin, viola, cello, and other string instruments (and are considered basic techniques on those instruments), but are significantly more difficult on the piano, where effort is required to develop a tremolo that is soft, smooth, and musical.

Connecting Passage

We now move on to the next few bars. Here, we have sforzando octaves over a piano accompaniment, giving it the quality of a declaration or a flash of thunder, before the right hand tumbles down in a virtuosic arpeggio. This is repeated twice before we begin to transition to the next part of the exposition using the first theme, stated above. Some sources state that this part constitutes a second "first theme" or perhaps a "subsidiary theme", while others state that this is just a connecting passage: I am more inclined to believe that it is not a theme, but rather a connecting passage, but each person can come away from this piece with a very different conclusion.

After that, we have yet more recurrences of theme 1.1, sightly modified, this time without the descending tail: instead, it ascends higher and higher, pulled down by sforzando chords every 4 bars. Given that the second theme ends in G minor, the dominant of C, we have a rise in G major from bars 35 to 37, a rise in A-flat major from bars 39 to 41, and a rise in B-flat major from bars 43 to 45, readying for a possible second theme in E-flat major.

Theme 2.1

Nope! It turns out the second theme is instead in E-flat minor, which, while sharing the same root as E-flat major or being "parallel" to it, is different— it still sounds sad, especially when compared to the next few parts. This harmonic surprise, no matter how small, reveals 2 things that will recur in Beethoven's works: the first would be the quick switch between the major and the parallel minor, and the second would be the harmonic surprises that constantly recur in his works and capture the attention of the listener. For convenience, I will refer to this theme as theme 2.1: the first theme of the second theme group.

With regards to the music itself, it is also more orchestral than pianistic: this is obviated by the constant hand-crossings in the right hand, which is evidenced by the (also constant) clef changes. This gives it the effect of a duet, with a bass voice around low C or in the octave above C3 and a soprano voice around high C or in the octave above C5. Furthermore, the soprano voice is heavily ornamented, with sforzando accents and grace notes embellishing the highest note in the upward arpeggio and mordents ornamenting the downward staccato runs. This, combined with the held note-chord-chord-chord accompaniment and the instruction to play softly give it a more light and elegant quality in the hands of a skillful pianist. I think this is the right time to mention the fact that the instruction "sforzando" means different things in different contexts: in a forte context, it can mean to play fortississimo, but in a piano context, it can mean just playing mezzo-piano or just with an accent, a sentiment also echoed by Schiff. So I believe this is a good place to have softer sforzandos as compared to the previous themes.

From bars 51 to 62, we stay in the tonality of an E-flat minor, doing either sforzandos or mordents. In bar 63, we begin to move to D-flat major and restate the second theme. After that, in bar 75, we remove the sforzandi in the soprano (moving them instead to the base) and use the mordent tail of the theme to modulate through to E-flat minor, F minor, C minor, B-flat major, and then back to E-flat major, according to James.

Theme 2.2

Here, we have another theme, this time in E-flat major, which is the relative major of C-major (so called because it has the same number of flats and sharps as the minor). Of course, sources disagree whether this is a third theme or part of the second theme group— but for convenience, I'll just mark this as theme 2.2, the second theme of the second theme group. Editors of Wikipedia state that this theme is very much like an Alberti bass. It begins at the home tone of E-flat major with the bass figures in the inner voices, and stays mostly still for some 4 bars: it then moves up quarter-note by quarter-note with the right hand and left hand moving in contrary motion, crescendoing until it reaches the e-flat an octave up. After this, we get 2 forte broken chords, much like an orchestral tutti, representing an IV-V-I cadence (a type of cadence that begins on the 4th scale degree, goes to the 5th or the dominant, and back to the 1st or the tonic: this is a very common cadence), bringing us back down for the pattern to be repeated or leading to the next part.

Cadential Theme

We now reach a large, virtuosic run down from the double-high E-flat to the G in the middle range. Unlike the previous connecting passage, this does not use arpeggios to reach downwards but instead uses scales, which prolongs the descent for much longer: and on the left hand, we have an I-vi-IV-V chord progression, a common chordal system not only in classical music but also in pop music as is explained by Longdon (2019). However, in Beethoven's hands, we also get some variation, as in the repeat of this part the chords change from the held note-chord pattern to an octave-chord pattern, in ever-more descending ranges. Musicians argue whether this theme is cadential, meaning its only function is to end the piece, or whether it is theme 2.3: I'll just side with Kumar (2017), as previously, on this topic and call it the cadential theme. After that, we repeat theme 1.1 in the key of E-flat, as with theme 2.2, and go to the repeat (which I have omitted from the discussion, for the sake of brevity).

But now, the question asked previously comes into play: Is the Grave an introduction, or is it part of the exposition? If you believe that the Grave is only part of the introduction, the repeat will only go back to the Allegro, which would be the top of the exposition. The first edition agrees with this view, placing the repeat sign at the tempo marking, and this is the view predominantly taken by most pianists. However, if you believe that the Grave is part of the exposition, you would instead repeat back to the top of the entire piece, essentially lengthening the first movement to dwarf the other 2 movements. This makes the size of the first movement comparable to the size of the second and third combined, and is the view taken by such pianists as Andras Schiff (who is a primary source for this essay) and by Rudolf Serkin. But regardless of your opinion on this matter, you would then repeat through all the previous sections and go back here, and then proceed to the beginning of the development.

Development

As previously stated, the development is when we begin to play around with the themes, earlier presented, in different harmonies and key colors. As a short review, there are five themes: the introductory theme, theme 1.1, theme 2.1, theme 2.2, and the cadential theme.

Restatement of Introduction

The development, interestingly, does not begin straight away with the themes but instead goes back to the Grave and restates the fortepiano chords. This restatement of the introduction, along with the restatement later on close to the Coda, is unique to this piece— James states that this reminds us of the aggrieved context to which the Allegro is a response. In any case, it begins in G minor in bar 133 and it moves up to E-flat in the next: but instead of moving up again in bar 135, it stays on E-flat and uses its enharmonic quality to D-sharp to move to E minor. This usage of enharmonic notes is by no means unique to this piece: a similar occurrence happens with the Appassionata Sonata, which is studied in depth in the dissertation thesis of Reale (2019).

Now, before continuing, I believe we ought to note the fact that we have moved to E minor, a key with 1 sharp, from C minor, a key with 3 flats: these 2 keys are very far away from each other, and so this is a real harmonic surprise. Beethoven's ability to bring us to keys very far away from the home key and back in a very natural way is something that recurs in all of his sonatas, not just this one: I will try to point out this unique occurrence whenever we see it. But onwards we go, to the development!

Alternation of Theme 1 and Introductory Theme

We begin with Theme 1.1, the Mannheim Rocket, in the key of E minor— the same key that we modulated to in the introductory passage earlier. However, this time, it is accompanied by a great crescendo, which gives it a more agitated and explosive character than the more restrained versions we have heard earlier. After this, we have the introductory rising-falling theme, also in E minor, played piano or softly. James remarks that this gives us the 2 views of suffering: there is the agitated, fighting side, and the depressed, suffering side of pathos— a wild swing that the sonata reflects with its themes. The combination of these motives is repeated, this time in the key of D major, which gives it a slightly more desperate feeling as it sinks lower.

Restatement of Theme 1.1 in Bass

We now continue with the roles of the hands reversed: this time, the left hand is playing a reduced version of Theme 1.1, without the tail and without the lower voice, as the right hand plays the tremolos that used to be in the bass. Even as the left hand tries to go up and up and up, the music overall chromatically descends: each motif is that of a descending semitone, and the tremolos descend every 4 bars from B-flat to A and then to A-flat. After 12 bars of this relative stability, the tremolos begin to fall and fall from the G in the middle register down to the B of the double-low register, nearly 2 octaves in span. As with many of the developments of Beethoven in minor keys, this passage is very unstable and very chromatic, with unclear harmonies wafting in the air.

I believe this is now a good time to take a closer look at Theme 1.1, and the reduction of it given here. The reduction here only has the top notes of the theme, and we can see that it descends by a semitone every 2 notes: if we look back at the modified statement of Theme 1.1 that was used to connect to Theme 2.1, we see that it is always composed of a descending semitone somewhere in the chord: this is not wholly audible, however, because there is always an ascending semitone as well, and the lower and upper voice trade-off these parts. When it is isolated and purified, as here, we see that it is built off a device that James states is used to convey weeping or suffering. However, I would remark that while it may convey a more doleful air when slowed down, I believe that when sped up to be this fast, it gives a feeling more of thinned anger and small, pinpoint bursts that do nothing to abate it rather than of sorrow.

Dominant Pedal

Now, if we look at the sheet music, we're only around halfway through the development: 5 lines have passed, and there are still 5 lines left to go. But in reality, we have reached the dominant: if you can read the first note of the tremolo, it is clear that the note is G, which is the dominant of C. It is also in a tremolo in the left hand, which makes it a tremolo drone, which gives it a similar status to that of an organ-point, of a long held-note in the left hand. In the classical period, this would signal the end of the development: for example, in Mozart's A minor piano sonata, K. 310, the dominant stays only for 6 measures before resolving back to the recapitulation. Indeed, one of Beethoven's hallmarks would have been his ability to extend the dominant tone for very long periods of time, and it is a pattern that we will see later on.

With regards to the music itself, we see that it is composed of two four-bar phrases. The first has low arpeggios, very close to the tremolos in the left hand, played at a pianissimo level or very quietly. These arpeggios are very unstable, and they move chromatically up and down, unable to decide which chord to strike: if we identify pathos, as suffering and rage, this is certainly on the suffering, depressed, wavering side. The second phrase has a modified, 2-bar form of Theme 1.1 shoot upwards from middle E to double-high E: unlike the modified forms of theme 1.1 as we have seen them earlier, though, this form has a descending tail, in this case composed of a trill. Here, the trill is used more in a classical sense, of being used to resolve back to the G: however, in later sonatas, Beethoven would use the trill in more unique and unusual ways, especially in his late-period sonatas with the Hammerklavier. This form of the Mannheim rocket is accompanied by a crescendo all the way up to the loud sforzando at the top of the phrase, which gives it the angered nature of pathos. These 2 phrases are repeated twice before Beethoven focuses on the trilled ending and uses a huge downward scale, reminiscent of the cadential theme, to reach the C in the very lowest register of the old fortepianos. And thus, we move to the recapitulation!

Recapitulation and Coda

Restatement of Theme 1.1

Now, something that may not have been obvious when I introduced Sonata Form on the landing page would be the fact that it is not only the second theme but also the first theme that changes. The recapitulation is not just a restatement of the theme, especially not in Beethoven’s time when thematic development was king: instead, we see how the Development section, how the battle between little fragments of the theme and big fragments of the theme in all different keys affects the motifs when they come out of the blender. In this case, I think the entire recapitulation is now dripped and soaked in the pathos that permeated the development: every theme is now in a minor key, all dripped with the sorrow and anguish expressed in the development. And so, we begin with the first theme.

In this case, Theme 1.1 is repeated twice, without any changes to its structure in the first repetition. However, in the second repetition, we get an entirely different tail of the theme: instead of remaining in C minor, as the head does, it modulates to D flat major on the first fall, E flat minor on the second fall, and then finally to F minor on the third fall, when it prepares to move on to the next theme. Now, there are 2 things to note here: the first would be that the passage of a sudden sforzando chord with a falling arpeggio that occurred in the exposition has disappeared, which is a significant indicator that it is not a theme but rather just a passage or at best a “subsidiary theme”, as per James. The other thing to note is the way the first theme transitions to the second: while in the exposition, we used the head of the theme to transition, going from G to A-flat to B-flat, we now use the tail of the theme to transition from C to D-flat to E-flat, which gives us a very pleasing symmetry: both have rising roots, but the exact way these are expressed changes. Indeed, music analysis gives us insight into the hidden workings of a piece in ways that are difficult to determine on the first pass. Regardless, we now move on:

Restatement of Theme 2.1

We now have the second theme. But wait— it’s not in C minor, but instead it’s in F minor! This is a smaller harmonic surprise, albeit F minor and C minor are far more related (F is the subdominant of C) rather than the E flat major and C minor of previously. And indeed, after the first statement of Theme 2.1 in F minor over bars 221 to 229, it begins modulating to C minor over bars 229 to 232 and settles the second half of the theme in bar 233, or the second line, 6th bar from the right. It now stays in C minor, the expected key, until bar 245 when uses the mordent tail of the second theme to modulate to F, and then to B flat, then E flat, then A flat, then takes advantage of the neighboring tone to move right back to G— preparing us for Theme 2.2, in the key of C-minor.

Restatement of Theme 2.2

Theme 2.2 is slightly unusual in that it is not just a direct transposition of the theme into the key of C minor: instead, while the beginning and end are changed to align more with the minor tonality, the middle itself is directly copied from the first statement in the exposition. If we look at the first 4 bars when the theme is stable, both the right hand and the left hand move away from the C-minor Alberti bass and pull outwards: thus, when the contrary motion begins in bar 257 or in the first line, 5th bar, we see that the right hand jumps to e natural and the left hand goes up rather than down to b-flat to mimic more the statement of the theme in the exposition. This makes it significantly more interesting as compared to a direct transposition, which I feel sounds fairly bland with the repeated C-minor chords. Furthermore, instead of changing on the second repeat of this theme, it instead repeats it perfectly: maybe this is already because it was good enough to transition to the next material, which is also in C minor. With respect to the music itself, instead of the glorious and hopeful feeling that was evoked with the E-flat major phrase earlier (E-flat major is often associated with heroic music, like Tchaikovsky’s 1812 Overture or Beethoven’s Eroica Symphony), we get an anticipatory phrase, threatening the possibility of danger with the minor melody humming over a minor background: a danger that closens with every note.

Restatement of Cadential Theme

We now move on to the cadential theme, which is also in C minor: its light and airy quality are still present, but now tinged with the pathos that permeates the recapitulation. After it tumbles down from the double-high C to the middle E-flat twice, we get a repetition of Theme 1.1, this time with the player shouting at forte, until we reach 2 fortissimo diminished seventh chords— a type of chord, built on the seventh scale degree (e.g. in the key of C major, the note is B), that has a lot of dissonance because of the tritone that serves as its building block. Both of those chords signal the end of the recapitulation and the beginning of the coda.

Coda

For the third or fourth time, depending on whether you repeated the Grave as stated above, we have another slow passage. But instead of being punctuated by the forte-piano chords which signal the tonality of the motif, we get dead silence: an extraordinarily dramatic silence, in any case. Gone now are the dissonant shouts and refusals, and what is left is the soft, suffocated, depressed rising-falling motif. This now begins by using the material in the third bar of the introduction, of a cadence from F sharp, diminished seventh, to G-minor. Then we move up by a fourth to another rising falling passage, moving from B, diminished seventh, to C minor. Lastly, we get another diminished seventh chord, but the phrase stops before it is able to resolve and instead sinks down and down and down until it reaches the double-low octave and moves without pause to the ending.

After the Grave, we get one last recurrence of the theme in its original form: starting from a soft piano, it ascends as softly as it did in the exposition. But instead of descending, as with the exposition, it stays up and crescendos to fortissimo with the bass descending chromatically. And thus the movement is finished: with 5 fortissimo block chords, shaking the fortepiano of Beethoven’s day and even some modern pianos, and concluding with the final, perfect v-i cadence.

Concluding Remarks

Even this early in Beethoven’s music, we already begin to see the hallmarks of his playing: an extended development and coda, extended dominant preparation, stark contrasts between the loud and soft, the ability to modulate from nearby keys to far away and then back home, and the motivic economy. Each of these facets plays a greater and greater role as Beethoven matures and enters his middle and late periods: each will be transformed in new ways as he produces monumental work after monumental work. But even so, I believe it is a worthwhile endeavor to study the young Beethoven so that when we see his middle and late works, we will have the context to be able to say whether it is new, or whether it is a developed form of something that appeared earlier on. Thank you for reading!
