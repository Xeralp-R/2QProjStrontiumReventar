Grande Sonata Pathétique

Sonata No. 8; Op. 13; 1st Movement

Introduction

The Sonata No. 8, Op. 13 was composed by Beethoven in 1798 and was published in the year 1799. It was dedicated to Prince Karl von Lichnowsky, a good friend and artistic patron of Beethoven. It is unknown whether it was Beethoven or his publishers that gave it the nickname "Pathétique" (meaning full of emotion or sorrow or "pathos") but it is known that Beethoven found the name acceptable, at the very least.

Even in Beethoven's day, this was a very famous and popular sonata: James (2016) states that it was so popular that arrangements for wind nonet, string quartet and quintet, piano quartet, theatre orchestra, and other such musical ensembles have been made throughout the years. It captures emotion in a way that few pieces thence did, and seriously propelled the musical zeitgeist forwards from the Classical Era into the Early Romantic Era.

Much as previous sonatas were, the first movement is the most prominent, being in sonata form and taking the most time of all the movements. It also contains much of the pathos or emotion in the whole movement, and is in the key of C minor or 3 flats. The second movement, not discussed for the sake of brevity, is the slow movement in Adagio Cantabile, or "slowly and in a singing style": it is in the key of A-flat major, with 4 flats. Lastly, the third movement, also not discussed, is a Rondo, and is in the home key of C minor. I believe this sonata both exemplifies our understanding of traditional, classical, sonatas, while also displaying Beethoven's style of writing and thinking.

The first movement is quite long compared to most sonatas of the time, clocking in at 310 measures or around 8 pages in most editions. As with most other sonatas of the time, this first movement is in a definite sonata form, albeit we already begin to see the introduction and the coda that are usually not so much a part of the textbook form.

The score was taken from the Wallner Edition, which was published by Henle Verlag in 1976. That edition is an urtext edition, so while the author believes it is legal here in the Philippines, I am not so sure about the situations in other countries. The same applies for the recordings which I have duly cut up into the component parts: These were made by Fritz Jank in 1962.

Introduction

The sonata begins with a huge C-minor chord, with all hands playing some seven notes: even from the beginning, the piece evokes grandeur and sorrow, like few others before. James states that this chord would have shaken many fortepianos to their cores in Beethoven's day with their sheer weight. After this, the melody suddenly softens and goes up 2 steps, repeats the uppermost note, and then goes down 1 step. This rising-falling motif is important: it reappears later on, both in the introduction and also in the development, some 100 bars away. The constant recurrence of this motif is the reason why Andras Schiff states that this part is no mere introduction, but instead constitutes the first theme in the exposition: whether you agree or disagree with this view is up to you, but it will have effects later on.

Now, before we go on to the rest of the bars, we should take note of 2 things. First, the tempo is not Allegro, as was common with most first movements of the time, but instead is Grave. These tempi lie on the opposite ends of the spectrum of speeds: while Allegro means "lively" upon being translated from Italian, and is related to words like "alacrity", you don't have to know Italian to understand that Grave means playing gravely, slowly, and solemnly. This tempo difference, especially compared to the material 10 measures on, is why most consider this part just to be an introduction: for example, another introduction like this lies with the Les Adieux Sonata, which I discuss elsewhere. Second, the dynamic marking is highly unusual: fp, or forte-piano, means to play loudly and then suddenly softly. However, this effect is impossible on the piano: a note will decay for however long you hold the note and will disappear upon releasing the note, meaning that there is no way to play the same note softly. Of course, skilled players like Schiff have found a workaround, by repeating the note softly while releasing the pedal holding the loud note (albeit I am not entirely sure if this is correct, because I unfortunately did not see the lecture with my own eyes)– but, even so, it is somewhat unusual and unpianistic. This often leads people to say it feels more like a piano reduction of a symphony or a reduction of a piano concerto rather than a piano sonata— which may be the reason why it has been arranged for other instruments so many times.

Moving on, we see that the forte-piano chords and the rising-falling motif are repeated in the second measure, but this time moved up— the same occurs with the third bar, with the motif repeated twice. Each time the forte-piano chord recurs, it becomes more and more dissonant, liberally using tritones to evoke the gut-wrenching characteristic of this part, until it reaches the end of 4th measure and calms slightly with a scalar passage reminiscent of the Fantasias of Bach.

We now move on to the 4th to 10th measures. We can view it as something of a drama between 2 characters: the first has a pleading, softer voice, while the second has a voice of refusal, anger, and strength. This is reflected by the usage of different registers and dynamics of the piano. The angrier voice has a range somewhere between middle C and high C, which is close to the range of a tenor, albeit the lowest notes approach the bass range. It also always plays at a fortissimo dynamic, which means to play very loudly. On the other hand, the pleading voice has a range from high C to the C the octave above that, which approximates the range of a soprano. It also plays piano, or softly. So I think this is a fair representation of that.

With regards to the music, both use the same melodic idea, the rising-falling motif: this idea occurs both in the higher register and in the lower register, but slightly altered and with the harmonies filled in. On the left hand, we have a chromatically descending bass: while the bass is usually punctuated by the fortissimo chords, which obscure the descent somewhat, it is very clear from bars 7 to 8, when it constantly descends as it is counterbalanced by the ascending right hand until the melody peaks at bar 9.

In bars 9 and 10, the music thins significantly, with many dramatic pauses: the left hand comes down octave by octave and pulls the right hand down with it. In the second half of measure 10, we get a soft chromatic scale running down 2 octaves (this scale is not supposed to be showy nor virtuosic, but rather deflationary, according to James) until we reach the A flat, and proceed to resolve to the Allegro. Note that the transition between the 2 parts is sudden, without pause: contrast is one of the hallmarks of a Beethoven Sonata, and here the contrast would be between tempos, as elsewhere it may be with the dynamics.

Exposition

The allegro begins with the first theme, what is referred to as a "Mannheim Rocket": a huge, ascending, arpeggio continuously going upwards and upwards until it reaches its peak 2 octaves later. This technique features prominently in the Mannheim school of composition: a notable example would be with the finale of Mozart's 40th Symphony. However, while the natural tendency would be to crescendo over the upward run, Beethoven instructs the performer to play softly– this evokes the feeling of tension rather than a violent outburst, albeit those will invariably come later. After running up for some 4 bars, it begins to sink back down, loudening more and more until the repetition of the phrase. This 8-bar phrase composes the first theme of the sonata, and we will be playing around with it more a little bit later. For convenience, I will call this Theme 1.1.

Now, I think it's somewhat important for me to mention the left hand: it is playing notes with a tremolo effect, rapidly moving from one note to the other. This is yet another unpianistic effect in this sonata: tremolos are very easy to do on the violin, viola, cello, and other string instruments (and are considered basic techniques on those instruments), but are significantly more difficult on the piano, where effort is required to develop a tremolo that is soft, smooth, and musical.

We now move on to the next few bars. Here, we have sforzando octaves over a piano accompaniment, giving it the quality of a declaration or a flash of thunder, before the right hand tumbles down in a virtuosic arpeggio. This is repeated twice before we begin to transition to the next part of the exposition using the first theme, stated above. Some sources state that this part constitutes a second "first theme" or perhaps a "subsidiary theme", while others state that this is just a connecting passage: I am more inclined to believe that it is not a theme, but rather a connecting passage, but each person can come away from this piece with a very different conclusion.

After that, we have yet more recurrences of theme 1.1, sightly modified, this time without the descending tail: instead, it ascends higher and higher, pulled down by sforzando chords every 4 bars. Given that the second theme ends in G minor, the dominant of C, we have a rise in G major from bars 35 to 37, a rise in A-flat major from bars 39 to 41, and a rise in B-flat major from bars 43 to 45, readying for a possible second theme in E-flat major.

Nope! It turns out the second theme is instead in E-flat minor, which, while sharing the same root as E-flat major or being "parallel" to it, is different— it still sounds sad, especially when compared to the next few parts. This harmonic surprise, no matter how small, reveals 2 things that will recur in Beethoven's works: the first would be the quick switch between the major and the parallel minor, and the second would be the harmonic surprises that constantly recur in his works and capture the attention of the listener. For convenience, I will refer to this theme as theme 2.1: the first theme of the second theme group.

With regards to the music itself, it is also more orchestral than pianistic: this is obviated by the constant hand-crossings in the right hand, which is evidenced by the (also constant) clef changes. This gives it the effect of a duet, with a bass voice around low C or in the octave above C3 and a soprano voice around high C or in the octave above C5. Furthermore, the soprano voice is heavily ornamented, with sforzando accents and grace notes embellishing the highest note in the upward arpeggio and mordents ornamenting the downward staccato runs. This, combined with the held note-chord-chord-chord accompaniment and the instruction to play softly give it a more light and elegant quality in the hands of a skillful pianist. I think this is the right time to mention the fact that the instruction "sforzando" means different things in different contexts: in a forte context, it can mean to play fortississimo, but in a piano context, it can mean just playing mezzo-piano or just with an accent, a sentiment also echoed by Schiff. So I believe this is a good place to have softer sforzandos as compared to the previous themes.

From bars 51 to 62, we stay in the tonality of an E-flat minor, doing either sforzandos or mordents. In bar 63, we begin to move to D-flat major and restate the second theme. After that, in bar 75, we remove the sforzandi in the soprano (moving them instead to the base) and use the mordent tail of the theme to modulate through to E-flat minor, F minor, C minor, B-flat major, and then back to E-flat major, according to James.

Here, we have another theme, this time in E-flat major, which is the relative major of C-major (so called because it has the same number of flats and sharps as the minor). Of course, sources disagree whether this is a third theme or part of the second theme group— but for convenience, I'll just mark this as theme 2.2, the second theme of the second theme group. Editors of Wikipedia state that this theme is very much like an Alberti bass. It begins at the home tone of E-flat major with the bass figures in the inner voices, and stays mostly still for some 4 bars: it then moves up quarter-note by quarter-note with the right hand and left hand moving in contrary motion, crescendoing until it reaches the e-flat an octave up. After this, we get 2 forte broken chords, much like an orchestral tutti, representing an IV-V-I cadence (a type of cadence that begins on the 4th scale degree, goes to the 5th or the dominant, and back to the 1st or the tonic: this is a very common cadence), bringing us back down for the pattern to be repeated or leading to the next part.

We now reach a large, virtuosic run down from the double-high E-flat to the G in the middle range. Unlike theme 1.2, this does not use arpeggios to reach downwards but instead uses scales, which prolongs the descent for much longer: and on the left hand, we have an I-vi-IV-V chord progression, a common chordal system not only in classical music but also in pop music as is explained by Longdon (2019). However, in Beethoven's hands, we also get some variation, as in the repeat of this part the chords change from the held note-chord pattern to an octave-chord pattern, in ever-more descending ranges. Musicians argue whether this theme is cadential, meaning its only function is to end the piece, or whether it is theme 2.3: I'll just side with Kumar (2017), as previously, on this topic and call it the cadential theme. After that, we repeat theme 1.1 in the key of E-flat, as with theme 2.2, and go to the repeat (which I have omitted from the discussion, for the sake of brevity).

But now, the question asked previously comes into play: Is the Grave an introduction, or is it part of the exposition? If you believe that the Grave is only part of the introduction, the repeat will only go back to the Allegro, which would be the top of the exposition. The first edition agrees with this view, placing the repeat sign at the tempo marking, and this is the view predominantly taken by most pianists. However, if you believe that the Grave is part of the exposition, you would instead repeat back to the top of the entire piece, essentially lengthening the first movement to dwarf the other 2 movements. This makes the size of the first movement comparable to the size of the second and third combined, and is the view taken by such pianists as Andras Schiff (who is a primary source for this essay) and by Rudolf Serkin. But regardless of your opinion on this matter, you would then repeat through all the previous sections and go back here, and then proceed to the beginning of the development.
