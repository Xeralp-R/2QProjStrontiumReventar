Introduction

From the funeral March sonata written in the beginning of his middle period, we now jump to what can be considered the final middle-period sonata: the Les Adieux sonata, written in 1809 and 1810. This is unique among his sonatas in that it is explicitly programmatic or that there is a “hidden meaning” of sorts to the music he wrote, something that he experimented with in the 6th “Pastorale” symphony as well. The story behind this sonata is related to the Archduke Rudolph, who was a great patron of Beethoven (with important works such as the “Archduke Trio”, op. 97, the 4th Piano Concerto, op. 58, and the Missa Solemnis, op. 123, all being dedicated to him) and at the same time a great musician (Beethoven created for him a large number of cadenzas that are all somewhat difficult to execute). In 1809, due to Napoleon’s War of the Fifth Coalition, Vienna was lain under seige in the Battle of Wagram: because of this, the royal family had to leave for their safety, including the same Archduke. It with the feelings that Beethoven was experiencing around this time— those of loss and anxiety, and eventually the joy what the Archduke returned after the war— that led him to write this sonata.

The piece itself is in the mold of a traditional sonata: it has 3 movements, following the fast-slow-fast movement form. The first movement is in E-flat major, and is titled “Das Lebewohl” or “The Farewell”: it has an Adagio introduction followed by a healthy Allegro, much like how the Sonata Pathetique had a Grave introduction followed by an Allegro— so we see how Beethoven’s usage of introductions evolves throughout his oeuvre. The second movement is an Andante Expressivo in C minor, and is titled “Das Abwesenheit” or “The Absence”: it uses a few themes repeated throughout in key after key to express the cycle of emotions that the persona feels while the other person is absent that recur over and over again. This is then interrupted by the final movement in the home key of E-flat major, which is titled “Das Wiedersehen” or “The Return”, and is marked Vivacissimamente or “As lively as possible”: it is also in sonata form, and it expresses the joy of seeing the other person again in an almost-orchestral manner, with specific passages evoking a piano concerto and thus foreshadowing future “Concertos for Solo Piano”.

The score was taken from the Wallner Edition, which was published by Henle Verlag in 1976. That edition is an urtext edition, so while I believe it is legal here in the Philippines, I am not so sure about the situations in other countries. The same applies for the recordings which I have duly cut up into the component parts: These were made by Fritz Jank in 1962. All of these resources can be found in the International Music Score Library Project, ot the Petrucci Music Library.

Sonata Introduction

The sonata opens with its most important motif: the “Lebewohl” motif, which Beethoven actually marked with the words “Le-be-wohl” over the notes. The motif is composed of a pair of voices outlining a descending 3-2-1 motif, which starts on G and ends on E-flat. Both the particular rhythm and the notes will be used again and again in different contexts of the sonata, so I believe it’s well worth remembering. We should also note the left hand, which only enters in the third note: it plays a C, which recontextualizes the notes above and changes it from an E-flat major chord (which is the expected happy chord to end the motif) to a C-minor chord, which gives it a certain sadness and sentimentality brought by that surprise change in harmony. Lastly, it is often commented that this particular motif sounds like it would be a horn call: horns are noted for their soft, round, and gentle sound, which is something that many people feel is appropriate for this particular part. Jonathan Biss, a well-renowned pianist, noted that this marks an evolution in Beethoven’s capacity for writing orchestral parts for piano solo: where he feels that the drums and horn calls in the 3rd movement of the previous funeral march sonata only impede or interrupt the mournful feeling of the piece, here he notes that the horn calls at the beginning add meaning and emotion to the piece, which may mark a significant improvement on Beethoven’s part in writing orchestrally for piano.

After this “Lebewohl” motif, we see another: a rise of 3 notes, where the last 2 notes are higher than the first note. This motif, which I will call the “jumping” motif, is repeated 3 times in the first line and reaches ever-higher and more wistful heights. Compounded with the bass, which is falling chromatically (Schiff (2004-2006) refers to this as the “Passus Duriusculus”), and we see that this next bar feels very unstable, as if we’re searching for a center or floating unmoored. This downward chromatic movement, series of incomplete harmonies (which, in the formal terminology, are called half-cadences), and constant switching between major and minor and between registers of the piano continues on for another 3 bars before we reach bar 7, which has another instance of the Lebewohl motif, now more outlined, preparing us for a true cadence.

However, this is diverted yet again in bar 8, where we see that instead of resolving to E-flat major as we want to hear or in C-minor as we heard earlier, it resolves to C-flat major, a key with 7 flats and that is far away from either E-flat major or C-minor. It then continues on with the jumping motif in a progression very similar to the one we heard earlier but now in the new key, which gives it a slightly happier feeling and an entirely new color that is wholly different from the one we heard earlier— it feels at least slightly detached from the rest, like another person talking. We then have the unstable progression repeated again and again, with the bass always descending chromatically, which slowly darkens the mood until we reach Bar 12, and then rest.

Starting in Bar 12, we now get a new progression, which I think feels more like a natural extension of the previous bar’s lament as it grows more and more despondent. In particular, this passage is filled to the brim with rests: similarly to the Sonata Pathetique, Beethoven’s rests in particular are used to heighten the drama, and here they contrast with the constantly moving harmonies of the previous bars to emphasize the feeling of despair that permeates this passage. Other techniques can also be used to emphasize the emptiness: for example, Schiff uses a staccato attack to make it shorter, while Oppitz lifts the pedal early to interrupt the sound. In either case, the rests become more and more prevalent, until at the very end of the passage there is a rest after every note and the emotion reaches its height.

Exposition

Theme Group 1

Directly from the gaping octaves of the end of the Adagio, we get a huge statement of the octave in forte which hugely contrasts with the previous section and marks the beginning of the Allegro. We now get the first theme of this theme group, which I will call Theme 1.1: it is composed of the first four (4) bars of the Allegro, in the edition bars 17-20, and it is composed of two (2) figures plus the octave at the very front. The first figure has this rising pattern highlighted in red: I believe that it is directly taken as an extension of the “jumping” motif at the very beginning (Tovey suggests, however, that it might be accidental), with the left hand being an extension of the chromatic descents that also occurred and recurred over and over again at the start of the piece. The second figure is composed of rising legato octaves in the right hand (one of the many unique challenges this piece presents to the pianist) and diatonically falling thirds in the left hand, which I think feels very much like a natural extension of the first figure. Because of the fact that these 4 bars feel more like extensions of the previous segment rather than themes in their own right given that they are constructed from the same materials as the introduction, it can be difficult to feel like this is a theme at all: however, because of the fact that it recurs over and over again in the development section, I think it can be safely considered as a theme.

The next theme in this theme group comprises another 8 bars, from bars 21 to 28— as per the above naming system, I will refer to it as Theme 1.2. The right hand is composed of octaves quickly changing register which sing this very beautiful tune laden with dynamics and expression. Even though, at its greatest, the right hand can jump as high as 2 octaves, I think it feels more like an opera singer using their full ambitus or range to express themselves rather than multiple voices chiming in to sing the part. I think that the right hand lends most of the sentimentality of this theme, because of the sheer fact that it refuses to resolve: it avoids resolution after resolution and keeps on hanging on to the dominant, b-flat major, which lends it more sentimentality and more drive. In the left hand, we see an arpeggiated accompaniment figure. Schiff notes that the left hand plays an inversion of the Lebewohl motif: the first lowest bass note is E-flat, then F, then G, much as the Lebewohl motif is G, then F, then E-flat: this particular progression is then emphasized or accented by most pianists, for example by Barenboim in the above video. However, Tovey notes that we need to be cautious about searching and perhaps over-highlighting all of the instances of the Lebewohl motif: he argues that it was intentionally added not necessarily for the listener to pay attention to it but rather for the subconscious to detect it and then associate it with the sad emotions of the beginning, to be an undercurrent of sadness to the excitability of the right hand. I believe that we should still emphasize the lower notes, but nevertheless take Tovey’s note into consideration, as his explanation lends credence to why this part still feels very sentimental even though we’re now going at a full allegro.

Transition

The transition comprises a full 21 bars: this is by far an absolutely gigantic transition, especially when compared to the first theme group which is only around half as long. This is in stark comparison to the Sonata Pathetique, where the first theme group and the transition are of about equal length, or the Funeral March Sonata, where the transition is around the same length as the B part. I think this is primarily due to the sheer economy of the first theme group: because so many of the ideas have already been exposed, the first group can be significantly shorter than usual, which means the transition feels oddly out of proportion. Tovey also notes that “one of the best ways of getting an effect of breadth in a short time is by devoting ample space to dominant preparation” (Tovey, p.190-191): in this case, the harmonies mostly emphasize the dominant of the dominant, F, in order to prepare the second theme group which is in the dominant key. Lastly, the fact that it stays so long without resolving properly into the dominant combined with its fast speed gives it a breathless feeling, as if someone is trying to catch up to the person who is leaving to say goodbye or something like that. This is emphasized by the writing of the next theme group, when we have 2 voices interlocking. As for its structure, the Lebewohl motif permeates this entire passage, this time in diminution or in small note values: this transition can prove difficult due to the rather fast scales in thirds and in chords that can trip up the player.

Theme Group 2

After that, we have the first theme of the second theme group, Theme 2.1, in the expected key of B-flat major. The uppermost voice of the right hand very clearly has the Lebewohl motif, this time with an extra tail that allows the voice to wander for the duration of the third, longest, note. Beneath this, we have an extension of the eighth-note figures that we saw earlier in the lower right hand, which adds a little bit of difficulty: lastly, in the left hand, we have a syncopated chord progression (which may be based on the jumping motif of the introduction, but that’s probably accidental) that, when placed in contrast with the rather placid right hand, gives it a little bit of a jaunty character. After the statement of the Lebewohl in the double-high register 2 octaves above middle C, we get a repeat of the same statement in the high register 1 octave above middle C: I believe that this part feels like 2 voices repeating the same thing, and in particular the interpretation above makes it feel like 4 voices, with the top right and and the left hand contrasting each other. Together, the rich harmonization that constantly surrounds this part and its unique lyricism that stands out from the motivism of the previous sections makes it one of the most meaningful parts of the Sonata every time it reemerges.

After this, the three voices compress onto one staff, making these next few bars somewhat tricky to interpret. The interpreter above, Barenboim, emphasizes the lowest voice as it descends while he changes the upper voice to be playing accompaniment to give the illusion that the eighth-note figures from the previous theme are continuing, as does Schiff: Oppitz and Lortie, however, either allow the the bass voice and the top voice equality or alternate between emphasizing the bass voice and the top voice. In either case, the decision is for the interpreter to make. After those 4 bars, the music simplifies to two voices that begin repeating and passing off to each other (or “imitating”) the Lebewohl motif, first in diminution (again, in short note values) and then simplified to 2 notes, the second being longer than the first. After that, the music slows and softens to an almost complete halt, when we get the full Lebewohl motif in whole notes in the left hand but is then interrupted by the right hand after 2 bars to either prepare us for the return to the exposition or to move the harmony onward to the dominant.

Development

In the middle and late periods of Beethoven’s life, we begin to see more and more compression: the development is no exception to this general trend, with a significant number of harmonic and melodic changes all occurring, sometimes even with implied harmonies. While it can be divided any number of ways, I have elected to divide it into 2 parts, as per the work of Kumar (2017).

Part 1

The development starts with the connecting passage leading straight into a statement of only the head of Theme 1.1 in the key of C-minor. Right after this, however, we get a rather mysterious part which makes us feel unsteady or unmoored: statements of the (altered) Lebewohl motif, alone and unharmonized, alternate with huge chords and constantly-shifting statements of the jumping motif in the left hand. Another interpretation has it that the chord is actually the third, long, note of the Lebewohl motif with extra harmonization, albeit noticing this requires a certain balance to the chords that I have not yet heard. The fact that there is so much empty space in this theme compounded with the chromaticity of the chords makes this part feel very alone— it echoes the second movement’s constant use of the jumping theme to express loneliness. Interestingly, Tovey is able to make sense of the progression by extracting every chord and then demonstrating how the Lebewohl motif is both a natural extension of the chords and bridges gaps between the chords (p.192), albeit parsing it requires an advanced understanding of harmonic language that I do not have. In any event, it then reaches the dominant key of B-flat, where it repeats itself to mark its importance (and maybe heralding the beginning of the recapitulation) before slipping up chromatically and having Theme 1.1 enter in A-flat then fall into G-flat major before diverting into the second half of the development.

In my opinion, these 16 bars of development are all there to lead back to the recapitulation: much as the transition between Theme 1.2 and Theme 2.1 was extraordinarily long, so is this transition between the Development and the Recapitulation. At this point, everything becomes very slow and placid, almost despairing and lifelorn: everything is set in piano softening to pianissimo, and the right hand chords seem to be falling under their own weight. In the left hand, we just have the jumping motif constantly playing, like an ostinato, no longer adding to the bounciness of the theme but rather sputtering out and dying. The first four bars of this section have both the right hand and the left hand falling together: this first part uses enharmonic shifting to modulate from the G-flat major of the previous statement of Theme 1.1 into C minor, with Tovey highlighting the 2nd bar (Bar 95) in particular as being the one to shift from one key to the other. After those four (4) bars, the left hand settles of the note C instead of falling any further: however, the right hand just continues falling and falling. This goes on for another 6 bars before the left hand is simplified, the aforementioned “sputtering out”: it now just becomes a single broken octave, repeated twice. Before long, we settle on a single chord: C, E-flat, and A-flat, which, after 3 bars of tension, expands to the right hand, builds up in a technique called “stretto”, and leads us directly back to the recapitulation.

Recapitulation

I don’t think the recapitulation is of too much interest, given that it is an almost exact reproduction of the exposition. There are only a few differences between this section and the exposition. The least important changes happen to the transition and the second theme group: the most obvious one is that the passage is transposed to the tonic of E-flat major, but it may also include moving the passage 1 octave up, adding an extra note to fill out the octave, or removing an extra bar to modulate correctly. However, what I think is the most important change for the pianist to note happens in bar 118: here, the left-hand voicing is very slightly altered to have the “Lebewohl” motif occur in half notes and then stall for extra-long on the third note rather than in whole notes. According to Tovey, this change is intentional: the autograph shows that he wrote out this altered figure after crossing out the original version, the direct copy of Bar 25. Furthermore, this very slight, perhaps only subconscious change comes to fruition in the Coda, where a very similar passage in the left hand occurs close to the climax of dramatic action— thus, in my purview, it is not a good idea to correct it (for example, as Hans Bulow does in his “Instructive Edition”).

Coda

One of the characteristics that makes the Les Adieux sonata so unique is that its coda is so long: where the exposition only comprises one and a half pages, and the development comprises only 1 page, the coda comprises two and a half pages: The fact that the Coda is both so long and has so much thematic development (rather than just restatements of the theme, as in the first movement of the Sonata Pathetique or in the last movement of the Moonlight Sonata) sets it apart from the other sonatas of its day. Jonathan Biss notes that this emphasis on both the Coda and the Introduction was such a large innovation that it began to bend the rules of what can compose a sonata: he notes that this is only one in a long line of compromises, ranging from focusing more on the intervals of the third and the sixth rather than the fifth as in his late sonatas, to not having any movement in sonata form like the Funeral March Sonata, to having protracted and thematic intros and codas like this sonata, to removing the development altogether as per the 2nd movement of the Tempest Sonata. In this case, I think it’s better to think of the coda like an altered 2nd repeat of the recapitulation and then the actual Coda, which is somewhere at the end. Thus, we can divide it into 3: first theme group, second theme group, and ending.

Theme Group 1

The coda begins with 4 bars of transition: these mirror the last 4 bars of the Exposition, which lead either to its own repeat or into the Development. Of course, it can be argued whether this is actually part of the Coda or part of the previous Recapitulation: the sources I have read, however, mostly agree that this transition is part of the Coda. This passage modulates us to the interesting key of F-minor, on the second scale degree of E-flat major.

We then have both Theme 1.1 and Theme 1.2 in the key of F-minor. This is one of the most important moments in the whole sonata for both themes. The first theme changes so that there are no more held notes: combined with the fact that this occurrence is in a minor key rather than in the major keys of previous occurrences, I think this passage feels very desperate and yearning. The tail of the theme in particular is interesting: the changed contour of the notes due to the sudden inflection downward at the end really deepens and strengthens the emotions present in the music. However, I feel like the greatest transformation happens not to Theme 1.1 but rather to Theme 1.2— This particular instance of the theme, in my opinion, is the climax of the entire work: the desperation and yearning that has been roiling and building up is let go in this very moment, and the anguish and true wish to keep the other person from departing presents itself in its strongest form. Barenboim, in particular, has a very effective interpretation where he adds an extra pause after the 4 jumping octaves to contrast the jumping octaves with the tail of Theme 1.2. After this occurrence of Theme 1.2 in F minor, Beethoven prolongs the moment, in a sense, by providing half of a repetition in the key of E-flat minor: however, this repetition is interrupted by a modulation in what is supposed to be the second half of the theme. (This is where the previously mentioned “odd movement” of the left hand recurs yet again, this time being used to modulate.) At this point, we can say that the theme “explodes”: having reached its dramatic height, it disappears just as dramatically, in this case never to be seen again. Thus, all that remains is the Lebewohl motif and its dying out.

Theme Group 2

We begin with a transition from that “cliffhanger”, in a sense: yet again, we see that Beethoven uses the Lebewohl motif to bring us to the right key, this time without interruptions from chords but instead bringing us there via dialogue between the left hand and the right hand. It is this way that he is able to bring us back home to the tonic key for Theme 2.1. He prepares us for the entrance by using a false cadence in bar 195 on the mediant or on G, which is the exact same note that Theme 2.1 starts with.

When Theme 2.1 occurs (some 16 bars later, it should be noted), it is also changed: gone now are the jumping chords of the right hand and the trill-like accompaniment of the alto voice, and all that remains is the melody itself and a counterpoint in eight-notes. Especially in comparison to the rich accompaniment and full tone of the previous recurrences of Theme 2.1, this one feels more simple and sweet. After the first half of the theme, the Lebewohl motif moves into the left hand and the counterpoint into the right— the left hand is only able to go on for 2 measures, however, before repeating those 2 bars again in the middle octave and again in the high octave before falling down for another 2 bars. The two hands then repeat this dance, with only slight variations: the 4-bar theme perfectly in the right hand, then the same theme cut up again in the left hand and rising for 6 bars. However, instead of falling in 2 bars, it takes 4 bars to fall back down, this time reusing the short-long-short pattern of the left hand all the way back in the first occurrence of Theme 2.1 and softening to an almost complete halt.

Coda Proper

The first part of this coda recalls the transition from the First Theme Group to the Second Theme Group: it also has the Lebewohl motif deconstructed, albeit this time always in the key of E-flat major. This deconstruction goes on for 12 bars, and the meaning behind it is well-hidden, if it exists at all. Both Perahia (2019), in the introduction to his edition of the sonatas, and Agmon (1996) suspect that this part may be more psychological compared to anything else: the ultimate goal of these 12 bars is to get the left hand to state the Lebewohl motif both completely and both in the right key and register by repeating the Lebewohl motif over and over again for the first 8 bars then rising higher and higher (and diminishing the note-value of the first note from whole to quarter) for the next 4 bars. Once this “teaching” is complete in bar 234, the rest of the 21 bars are there to tone-paint: for example, the 4 bars directly afterward are the descending horn calls that mark the person’s departure and the 4 bars after that are like the sound of horses’ feet (and form the most compressed that we have seen the Lebewohl theme, now reduced to 2 quarter notes). After that, we see the left hand and right hand at their furthest apart, with the left hand playing a falling bass that reminds us of the Lebewohl motif, and the right hand playing extremely high scales in counterpoint: while previous points may have been the climax of drama, this is a significant climax of sentimentality, and I personally believe that this part, when rendered well, can always give goosebumps. (Agmon notes that at this point, we have an exact reversal of the introduction, with a C-octave in the right hand and the E-flat and G in the left, but instead of propelling the piece this fizzles out to a harmless B-octave. Schiff also notes that on the C-octave, a crescendo is written, making it impossible for the pianist. Lastly, Perahia notes that this is also tone-painting, if we think of it like the carriage of the archduke going on a hill or faraway mountain then disappearing.) The piece then ends on two forte staccato chords, perhaps indicating that the persona is trying to resist falling away into sentimentality and is resolved to stay happy even if the other person is leaving.

Conclusion

This piece is, in my opinion, one of the masterworks of sonata form. It is considered in the pantheon of the great works of Beethoven’s middle period: it ranks equal to the Waldstein Sonata and the Appassionata Sonata in this regard.

It is also the last idiomatically middle-period sonata of Beethoven. It was 5 years before he published another sonata, in 1815: this work is significantly different from his other works but it is unclear whether it belongs to the middle period or the late period, with Rosen, for example, placing it in neither. Indeed, this first movement may have just been saying farewell for a coming return: but somehow, it also marked farewell to the conventions of Sonata Form as we know it, and further forward we will now be seeing fugues, themes and variations, and sonatas with unclear themes and transitions. Beethoven’s music here becomes so different and so unique that even today, we have about as much difficulty parsing it as the people of Beethoven’s day did.

But still, this sonata is open to read. And indeed, its use of tone-painting, however reluctant in some cases, foretells the use of tone-painting by other composers: it most clearly leads directly to the tone-painting of Schumann and Grieg. And the sonata’s virtuosity still makes it a favorite for travelling pianists, which no doubt further adds to its popularity. Thank you for reading!
