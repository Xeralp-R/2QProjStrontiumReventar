Introduction

The Funeral March Sonata was composed in the year 1801, a year when Beethoven was experimenting with the many forms of classical music in his time: it was in the same year that he composed the Moonlight Sonata (I have elected not to give an analysis of this sonata because others have already done this work for me: please refer here or here, which was the product of mixing fantasies and sonatas, along with the Pastoral Sonata, which is also somewhat popular for its humorous and lighthearted nature. This was also 1 year before he wrote the Heiligenstadt Testament, as detailed in the introduction— so this work can be considered in closer nature to the middle sonatas rather than the earlier classical sonatas of Beethoven. Indeed, Schiff places the tipping point between the Early period and the Middle period, at least concerning Beethoven's Piano Sonatas, with the one right before this one, which is Opus 22, Piano Sonata No. 11 in B flat major.

The piece itself is in 4 movements, matching the mold of classical sonatas up until that time— however, the similarities with the past essentially end there. For one, if we look at its 4 movements, we see that none are in sonata form. Furthermore, the first movement is a theme and variations in the key of A-flat major: Schiff states that the only precedent for this movement would be the Mozart sonata in A Major, K331, which can be found here on YouTube, which also has a slow first movement in variation form and no movements in Sonata form. The second movement is a scherzo and trio, a type of light music in 3 parts, also in A flat major: while Beethoven wrote many scherzi before and many after, what is unique about this is that it is the second movement when it usually is the third movement of a 4-movement sonata. The same is true of the slow third movement, which usually goes second— this movement is the namesake Funeral March, which is in the parallel key of an A-flat minor. Lastly, we have a fast fourth movement in the usual rondo form. The unique thing about this sonata, according to Kumar, would be its seeming disunity, with the second, third, and fourth movements being completely incongruous to each other, both in mood and in tone color: instead, they are united by the first movement, which foreshadows each movement with one of its variations. Thus, merely by talking about this first movement, we already gain insight into all of the movements of this sonata.

Lastly, I think a short introduction to the Theme and Variations form should be in order, as the first movement of this sonata is set in that form. As the name suggests, this form begins with a theme, which is usually fairly simple and unornamented. There are then variations on that theme, each of which presents the theme differently: they may change the harmony underlying the theme to form what is called a harmonic variation, they may add ornaments or embellishments in an ornamental variation, they may change the melody wholesale but not the harmony in a melodic variation, or change both but still retain the primary elements is what is called a character variation. This first movement makes liberal use of character and ornamental variations: for examples of melodic variations, I would suggest a look at the Goldberg Variations, made by JS Bach.

Tema: Andante con Variazioni

AA Part

*Note: when counting bars, the first upbeat is ignored.

The theme begins with a regular 8 bar phrase, which is very lyrical and expressive— indeed, I would say that it is quite beautiful, perhaps slightly sentimental or melancholic. This phrase was so great that Franz Schubert, [an Austrian composer often considered as a great lyricist and lieder or song maker and a great admirer of Beethoven], is believed to have taken inspiration from the theme in his Impromptu, Op. 142/2 (see https://youtu.be/38-S2bRo5rM) and in his Sonata in C minor (see https://youtu.be/ltvKcZ9U5QA). Even in its base form, this theme already has some ornaments and embellishments, like the passing note and the turn in the fourth bar: these sorts of things are not uncommon in theme and variations form, and they serve to emphasize the lyric quality of the piece. After this, we repeat the same 8-bar phrase with a slight variation, namely, that the eighth notes in the first bar have been converted into sixteenth notes. This slight change is usually accompanied by an interpretational change in overall dynamic so that things remain interesting, as is the natural progression.

Before we move on, I would like to take note of a minor change that, while slightly less prominent and still present in previous sonatas, still heralds a major change in composition. If you take another look at the score above, we see a large number of instructions like staccatos, slurs, sforzandos, and crescendos. While nowadays, it is presumed that the composer will mark out every articulation or somehow indicate the articulation otherwise, many of the predecessors of Beethoven would instead presume that the player is intelligent enough to place these sorts of articulations in the correct locations, and would only write these things out for beginners. This was the especially prevalent mood in the Baroque period, where the composer would only have to write forte or the type of piece and would then expect the player to know the genre intimately enough to be able to make the articulations on the fly. However, starting with Beethoven, we get more and more articulations being directed to evoke a particular sound or mood, which may be contrary to the normal method of playing. This goal of Beethoven, to communicate more and more information to the player about how to play his works rather than leaving it to their discretion, is one of the trends that increase as Beethoven gets older, with his late works even having German tempo markings rather than Italian ones so that Beethoven can communicate exactly what he wants. This trend continues up to this day, especially in the period of computer-generated audio samples: in these cases, the computer will certainly not improvise nor interpret the music, which essentially lends the composer as much power over the music as he can realistically get.

BA Part

After this, we now get the second phrase, which is not in 8 bars but instead in 10: Schiff notes that this theme contrasts the symmetrical with the asymmetrical, a method of contrast that Beethoven also uses in his other works. For the first 4 bars, the melody stays in the bass voice while the upper voices play a countermelody in thirds: this melody is syncopated, or it falls on the upbeat of the previous bar rather than the downbeat of the current bar. This syncopation is retained when the melody is moved to the right hand in the fifth bar of this part, which is on the second line: it goes on a beautiful part-written fashion until we reach the seventh bar, last best when we hear a cadential trill in the soprano. But when this resolves on the eighth bar, there is still a sense of incompleteness, because the bass voice has ended on a C, which is the third above the tonic; so it repeats that last bar so that we get a proper resolution in A-flat. Repeating a purported "failed cadence" is something that also reappears in Beethoven's works— this can happen very elegantly, as in this sonata, or more brusquely, as in the trio in the scherzo of Beethoven's Hammerklavier, discussed elsewhere. After this, the first theme is repeated in the more ornamental form with only a few changes.

If we look at the structure, we see that we began with the first 8-bar phrase, repeated it, moved on to the 10-bar phrase, and then repeated the 8-bar phrase only last time. If we assign letters to these phrases, moving to a new letter whenever we get a new phrase, we get the structure AABA. This is the same form taken by many other themes in other similar works, most notably the first movement of Mozart's A major sonata and the second movement of Beethoven's own Kreutzer sonata, for violin and piano: in this sense, it is a very classical theme, and yet it already feels very Beethovenian. I think a key to that would be the use of wide multiphony and polyphony, even in this first movement: in Mozart's A major sonata, the theme is very clear, as both hands are playing the theme without accompaniment voices, and the chords at the end of the theme feel rather heavy; this work, however, already has four, five, or even six notes sounding at once, so a change to only 2 voices sounding in the B part feels very light in comparison. The other key might be the usage of the damper pedal: Beethoven’s works, such as this, are often played with great washes of pedal, with the Master himself indicating in other sonatas like the Waldstein to hold it for extreme lengths; Mozart’s pieces are more often played without pedal to give the notes a clarity that matches with the clarion tunes that are played in that manner. Combined, we see many of the hallmarks of Beethoven’s skill with slow tunes appearing in this relatively early work. Onwards to the first variation!

Variazione I

AA Part

The first variation begins by having a variation on the A part and then repeating that variation: this keeps in line with the AABA structure of the theme, which is also present in this variation. The theme itself features “quartal writing”, or rather it feels like it is better scored for a quartet: the reason for this is because it makes use of the different registers of the piano which, while fairly close to each other, more closely match that of a string quartet. Quartal writing is also present in many of Beethoven’s other sonatas. It also makes use of a pattern for each melodic line or phrase: an ascending run of 32nd notes, followed by 2 eight notes, which give it a constant rhythm that switches from exerting effort to relaxing a little. In Bar 35, this rhythm is used in an upper bass context, giving it the feel of a viola: this also occurs in Bar 37, and Bars 41 and 43 on the second line. In the other bars, this rhythm is used in a low treble or a high treble context: these feel like the first and second violins switching around their use of the melody. Lastly, to fill out the quartet, the cello plays these bass notes (here filled out to be chords) to ground the harmony with rich tones as it did in the theme.

BA Part

We now move on to the B and A parts of the second variation. Beginning in the third line, counted as Bar 51, we see the right hand playing the same countermelody as it did in the theme, and the left hand playing the melody, this time ornamented and converted into the 32nd note form as we have seen before. We also see that Beethoven does not keep his rhythms static: he also makes variations on them, as in bars 51-52 and bars 53-54 on the next line, we see that what were once straight eight notes are now syncopated slightly, emphasizing the long silence between, and we now also have a downward tail in 32nd notes. Lastly, we also see carefully placed sforzandi on the apex of the figure, which emphasizes the interpretation where high notes and ascending figures are played louder than low notes or descending figures. If we had to assign an instrument to this melody, we could probably give it the upper register of a cello.

After those 4 bars, we get the 2 bars of new material: what were once straight octaves are now broken octaves, yet another technique that is idiomatic for violin as it emphasizes string crossings and thus the subtle differences in timbre between the strings. We then get the four-bar extended cadence, which runs into the third line of the above excerpt: the lowest voice plays fast upward staccato notes, reminiscent of an upbow or downbow staccato on the cello, as the upper voices play the corresponding cadential chords. And finally, after the transitional passage also reminiscent of string-crossings on the violin in bar 60, we repeat the A phrase in much the same manner as we did previously. Overall, we can say that this variation does not stray far from the first in tone or mood: instead, it feels like the natural extension of the first, much as classical variations were. We see that Beethoven was very well-acquainted with the forms of his time, which is what allowed him to begin pushing back on them.

Variazione II

AA Part

As you might have heard from the audio or seen in the score, almost the entire piece is played in staccato: rather than the string instruments of the previous variation, this one seems better suited either for woodwinds or the piano, as it can be difficult to coordinate instruments playing in such syncopated manners. Regarding the music itself, the melody has moved to the bass: it can be difficult to see, but if you can look at the circled notes in the following excerpt from the bass of bars 69 to 72 and this excerpt from the theme in bars 1 to 5 of the theme, we see that they have the same contour, and are just the same notes transposed down an octave.

The right hand now plays an accompanying figure, that being repeated staccato chords either in smaller thirds and fourths or (on the second repetition) in larger octaves: these chords are syncopated, which Schiff states adds a small element of humor to this variation. If we recall the statements I made in the introduction, this variation best mimics the scherzo, according to Kumar; the second movement of this sonata is also laden with staccato figures, and with some light humor, like the never-ending dominant preparation which begins in bar 25 and does not let down for another 20 bars, and the sudden “wrong entry” at the end of all that preparation. Thus, this variation can be taken to foreshadow that, in a sense.

Something we should note before continuing would be the fact that different people can play this variation very differently and thus cause it to feel very different. On the one hand, you can choose to play this variation very quickly, as Kovacevich and Barenboim do: it then sounds somewhat exciting and fun, as if one would be bouncing around and enjoying themself. On the other hand, you can choose to play this variation on the slow side, as Lewis and Buchbinder do: it then sounds a little bit more languid, like a jog in the park. I believe it sounds good in both cases, but you, the reader, will decide which one you prefer.

BA Part

Here, for the B part of this variation, the melody moves in the right hand: this makes it feel even more syncopated, as now none of the melody notes fall on any of the downbeats of the measure. It also retains its previous voicing, so to speak, with only 1 line of notes— now playing the decomposed form of the countermelody, they jump around in the left hand except at important points where they turn into full octaves. After that, we get a repeat of the A part with even more fully-voiced right-hand chords and a slightly changed melody in the left hand, all jumping around until the end.

We should note that this sort of pointillistic and deconstructionistic method is not uncommon in Beethoven’s other pieces in theme and variations form: for example, the second variation in his great Diabelli variations, Op. 120, are written similarly just with shorter note values to account for the faster tempo; something very similar occurs in the 2nd variation of his 30th sonata , which also decomposes its theme into small parts; and lastly, if we want to push the limit of deconstructionist tendencies, we can take a look at the first 3 variations of his 32 Variations in C-minor or variation 23 of the same set, which, while not keeping with the right-hand left-hand alternating pattern of this variation, still sound reminiscent because of the repeated notes (or chords) in the hand that it playing the variation on the melody. So this is most certainly not a bolt from the blue for Beethoven: this is only 1 of the techniques that he has in his arsenal and that he uses for his themes and variations.

Variazione III

AA Part

We have now arrived at the minor variation. In the classical period, a variation in the minor when the theme is in the major would be marked “minore”, meaning minor in Italian. And quite the minor it is! While A-flat major has 4 flats and is just on the border of what is considered easy to read by amateurs, A-flat minor has 7 flats, the most of any normal key: every note is flattened, which makes it quite a bit more difficult for amateurs to read. Indeed, before Beethoven’s time, key signatures with more than 4 flats or sharps were avoided because the tuning system of the day simply meant that these keys sounded terrible and dissonant: even when corrected with the equal-tempered tuning method that was already quite popular at the time, these keys were still avoided and were associated with death and lament, concepts which were also oft avoided. It was only really in the romantic period after Beethoven that these extremely flat or extremely sharp keys took hold, especially in the music of Chopin which became popular after Beethoven died. So, even starting with the key signature at the beginning of the piece, it already makes serious breakthroughs.

With regards to the music itself, I think it is quite obvious that the mood of this piece is very heavy: it evokes a variety of sad emotions which are difficult to place labels on, ranging from sorrow to pathos to anger. In the first A part, this sorrow is more reserved: the piano dynamic and the great washes of pedal hide and obscure much of the sorrow that is laden in the piece, along with the thinner voicing: this part can be played by just 1 hand that has the span of an octave, although thankfully Beethoven allows us to divide the part in 2. However, the second A part is much more sorrowful or perhaps ominous: the sforzandi pedal note all the way in the double-low or contra octave add a lot to the atmosphere of the piece, with Schiff calling them menacing or even threatening. These bass notes can even cover up the actual melody in the right hand, which makes them even scarier. In all, even just the start of this variation already heralds Beethoven's skill at composing in minor keys.

BA Part

If we take a look at the upbeat to the first bar of the B part, we see a B-double flat: the next notes are a chord composed of E-flat and G-flat. But we should note that the relationship between a B-double flat (or an A) and E-flat form a tritone, an extremely dissonant interval— in fact, it is considered the most dissonant along with the minor second, or the half-step. There is no doubt that tritones have been used very often before in music: they take part in the dominant fifth, which is a very common chord. However, the fact that in this sonata, they are being given serious prominence to even take up a melody position is something very groundbreaking and harmonically innovative, as Schiff puts it. So this piece really pushed our musical understanding forward on the possible uses of dissonances, and really reflects the romantic usage of dissonance. This continues on in the actual bar when the C-natural of the left-hand forms a tritone with the G-flat of the right, and goes on in the repetition of the phrase in bar 121 with the G-natural of the bass clashing with the D-flat of the treble. All of this dissonance gives this part great amounts of negative emotion: whether it be pathos or fear is up to the reader to decide. After that, we get a variation on the previous rhythmic pattern, with a 16th note on the 3rd beat contrasted by another sforzando 16th note on the upbeat: this really emphasizes the upbeat and strengthens the syncopations that were already there, making the music feel even more bent. This new rhythm continues on for 6 bars of the transitory passage until we get back to the home key of A-flat, upon which we restate the A part and fade away to nothingness.

As I previously mentioned, each variation in this set foreshadows a movement in this sonata. And while the previous variation had authors disagreeing, here essentially all sources that I have seen agree that this foreshadows the 3rd movement, which is the namesake funeral march. They have a large number of similarities: the A-flat minor key signature is the most prominent one, but also the liberal use of staccato (often overshadowed by the liberal and justified use of pedal by performers and interpreters), the great block chords in the right hand as the left hand plays countermelodies, even the use of sforzandi and fortissimi closely reflect each other. There is one final thing that both share: a climax that grows louder and louder, reaching its loudest on the dominant before suddenly petering out right then ending on a soft note, in a sense dying out. It’s difficult for me to say exactly when this practice began: Haydn’s Variations in F minor, which many believe had a lot of influence on Beethoven, seems to have a technique like this on a picardy third [a type of chord at the end of a piece where the chord is sharpened, making the last note major rather than minor], and Mozart’s Confutatis in his celebrated Requiem has something similar, but I think it was really Beethoven that began using this technique regularly and often (primarily because classical composers did not write a lot of pieces in minor keys) and his influence propagated the effects of this on essentially all romantic music, most notably with Chopin’s famous Prelude in E minor and Chopin’s own funeral march in his own sonata[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VUk3_qTTVg4], that took a lot of inspiration from this one. So all in all, I would say that the effects of this sonata are varied and widespread.

Variazione IV

AA Part

This variation is almost wholly different from the variation that preceded it, as the last one was to the variation that preceded it. In fact, this variation shares a lot of characteristics with Variazione II, in that both are light and humorous, rather than serious and grave. The feeling of this variation also heavily depends on the interpreter: Kumar states that when played slowly, it feels more ironic and funny, “like a heavy person trying to dance a ballet”, to use an odd metaphor; when played quickly, it feels like more a scherzo. As always, the choice of which interpretation you prefer is up to the reader.

With respect to the music itself, it is much like Variazione I in that it liberally uses the different registers of a piano, in this case in a more light way than the previous variations. While the register-writing for the first variation seems to be well-suited for the string quartet, this register writing seems to fit the woodwind section very well: the clarinet, oboe, and flute seem to be trading off the melody in the right hand, with the timbral differences allowing the syncopations to shine through that are difficult to hear on the piano; the bassoon, french horn, and perhaps the clarinet playing the left-hand countermelody, which also uses the different bass registers to great effect. As always, there are two A parts: the first A part has unison melodies running over a eighth-note bass: the second a part has the melody in octaves running over an interrupted 16th note bass. Lastly, we see the texture shift when moving from the first A part to the second, with the pianist being asked to play legato: so we see that Beethoven could use shifts in texture very well to keep the music from becoming stale.

BA Part

After that, we get the B part, where the first 4 bars are played in the same syncopated staccato manner. After that, the 6 bar transitory passage is played, with a series of syncopated resolutions, with the key chord always falling on the middle beat: I feel as though this part sounds especially jaunty, as the “resolutions” go up and up the keyboard’s register. This variation especially gives us insight into the harmonic structure of the theme: if one were to do a harmonic analysis of this piece (regrettably, I was unable to find one over the internet), this variation might be very useful in determining that. After that, we get a fast bass scale (which also has a slight element of humor, as the bass tones sound very clunky when attempting to play such a light scale so quickly), we repeat the A part with few changes from the second repetition, and then close this penultimate variation.

This variation is unlike the previous variation in one last way: while authors all agree that the previous variation most reflects the funeral march, authors disagree on whether this variation reflects the second movement, which is the scherzo that has already been compared to the 2nd variation, or the fourth movement, which is a wonderful rondo. I think the answer to this depends on the interpretation of the player: if this variation is played more slowly and the last movement also played in a graceful and slow-paced way, this might better match the last movement: however, if this variation is played more quickly, it will better match the second movement. Overall, I think that this variation best matches the second movement, in the light of Variazione V which best matches the final movement, but as always, the reader is free to draw their own conclusions.

Variazione V

AA Part

And so we come to the last variation. Much as Variazione IV used the same register-writing technique as Variazione I, only applied with a different mood and style, this variation uses the same deconstructionist technique as Variazione II: but this time, rather than the woodwinds that were playing the second variation, this one seems best scored for strings or string and orchestra, as best befits the finale to this set of variations. Indeed, the path of this set of variations is remarked to be elliptical: it goes out and out until it reaches the furthest from the theme, in Variazione III, the goes back closer and closer, reflecting everything that came before. And as the melody in the second variation was placed in the bass, the melody here is hidden in the alto voice: an analysis of the triplets in the first A part shows that it has a constant pattern, with a pedal note followed by a non-chord, embellishing tone (it’s difficult to say whether this is a passing tone or a neighbor tone) and then followed by the actual melody, while the second A part has the melody clearly marked with the second voice. You can see the diagram below to see the melody tones in the first A part, which have been highlighted— they are the exact same as in the first variation.

There is also another technique used in the first half of this final variation that differentiates the first A part from the second A part: while the first A uses triplet 16th notes, the second speeds up to using 32nd notes, which make it feel like the melody is climaxing or blossoming into something that I find quite beautiful. This part is usually played with a great wash of pedal and wonderful dynamics that only add the the beauty of the piece, and all add to the orchestral coloration of this piece— it really does feel like the whole orchestra is now playing this variation with great emotion and strength. Indeed, these sorts of textures were revisited and brought to fullness by the late Beethoven some 20 years later, in his 30th Sonata, Opus 109: there, we get to see the full progression of the theme in the final movement’s fourth variation, starting from its quarter-note chorale nature and progressing to eighth-notes, eight-note triplets, 16th-notes, 32nd-notes, and then finally reaching its climax at the trill, played as quickly as possible before dying down and restating the chorale theme.

BA Part

When we reach the B part, in order to distinguish it from the previous thickly orchestrated A parts, Beethoven writes more thinly: he first writes the melody in a higher register, which naturally cannot sustain as long as the bass tones on the piano, and then he writes in many rests, which give it a lighter feel compared to the weightiness of the previous part. After those first four bars, we get back to the six bar transitory passage, where he places a running 32nd-note counterpoint in the alto voice that outlines the melody quite well, in my opinion, and prepares us for the last restatement of the A part. Unlike previous variations, where this last A part was essentially the same as the second, this one has some key differences: while much of the accompaniment is the same, the melody is switched to the 16th note version, meaning that it feels fuller and faster than the second A part. And after that final run, we begin to rest and dial back: the alto voice returns to accompaniment duties, playing 16th-note chords over and over again as the bass plays a countermelody in staccato that Schiff remarks is much like the pizzicato of cellos and double-basses. And as we decrescendo from the climax in the last part, we hear a wonderful (and quite beautiful, if I do say so myself) melody in the soprano: it feels a little bit like a lullaby, slowly softening and softening until we reach the end of the piece, hit the final home chord played pianissimo or extremely softly, and go to sleep, marking the end of this first movement.

Conclusion

At the time, this sonata was extremely popular: it was one of the only sonatas by Beethoven that today we know have been publicly played for audiences, which was unusual at the time because sonatas were regularly only played for private audiences, as befitted the more intimate understanding of the classicists of the sonata. The funeral march in particular was an instant hit, even going so far as to be played at Beethoven’s funeral. It also inspired many other composers of the time: I have already mentioned Schubert, who was very much an ardent Beethoven fan, but it also inspired Chopin, who was more ambivalent and lukewarm about Beethoven: this A-flat major sonata was reportedly the only one Chopin played at his audiences and salons, and he liked it so much that it served as inspiration for his own 2nd sonata, which is also well-known for the funeral march in its third movement (the perennially popular Funeral March in B-flat minor, another extremely flat key). And there, we get to see the difference between Chopin and Beethoven: I feel like Beethoven’s funeral march is a lot warmer, because its cadences are strong and loud discounting the last, final cadence, which is somewhat eerie— all the cadences of Chopin, however, are soft, giving all of them the tragic quality that was only afforded to the last cadence in Beethoven’s march. But notwithstanding that, we see that it had a large effect on Beethoven’s contemporaries.

Regrettably, it is nowhere as popular now. Joachim Kaiser, an active music critic, notes that it might have been its elegant and sentimental character that made it excellent for the people living budding romantic period in which time Beethoven composed that make it more neglected today, being a victim of oversentimentality: much as the colder Appassionata sonata shines over the warmer Les Adieux, so it is with the colder Moonlight sonata, composed in the same year, over this warmer Funeral March sonata. However, I think it still has its virtues despite being a little oversentimental because it reflects a fonder view of life, one that, while marred by sorrow, still manages to look on the bright side. And that is why I consider it one of Beethoven’s seminal works even up to today. Thank you!
