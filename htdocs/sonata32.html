Introduction

This sonata was the last sonata Beethoven ever wrote: Op. 111, written some 10 years after the Les Adieux sonata in 1821. It was written as part of a trio with Op. 109 and Op. 110: however, unlike the trios of his youth, they are assigned different opus numbers and they were sold separately to set each of them as equal in importance to all of the others. All three works are solidly in the style of his late period, and each expresses a different concept or idea. All three works are also a huge break from the sonatas of the past, and all look forward to the future of music, this one being no exception.

The form of the sonata is still somewhat traditional, in a sense: it begins with a fast first movement in Sonata Form, and a slow second movement in Theme and Variations form. The second movement is particularly well-known because it seems to foretell the syncopated rhythms of boogie-woogie (albeit this is and has always been put under heavy scrutiny, given that there is little way by which Beethoven would have informed the African-American roots of that genre) and because its final variation is particularly enigmatic, not least because the theme is never completed in its final statement. The first movement is also also interesting in its own right, not least because the color of this movement is far darker than any movement Beethoven has written before: like a great castle of obsidian, it looms in the dark, edgéd and broken, haunting all. Due to that, at the very least, I think it is worth analyzing.

The score was taken from the Wallner Edition, which was published by Henle Verlag in 1976. That edition is an urtext edition, so while I believe it is legal here in the Philippines, I am not so sure about the situations in other countries. The same applies for the recordings which I have duly cut up into the component parts: These were made by Fritz Jank in 1962. All of these resources can be found in the International Music Score Library Project, ot the Petrucci Music Library.

Sonata Introduction

The sonata begins with this huge, declamatory statement: two octaves crashing down on each other, then two huge chords in the right hand. Together, they outline an F-sharp diminished seventh chord: this chord is very notable because every note in it is a tritone away from another note in the same chord. It is also notable for making an appearance in the Sonata Pathetique, as the first chord of the third bar— indeed, we can say that this sonata is very similar to the Sonata Pathetique in terms of color (both are dark, albeit this is really pitch-black) and somewhat in terms of form. In any case, we continue onwards with this rather stark trill (probably because the accompaniment has moved up to the middle register, so we don’t sense it as much as the very low bass notes of the previous bar) and then 3 large chords and a great arpeggiated flourish that finish on G-major, which is the dominant of C-minor. (It should be noted that there is a C-minor chord, but it doesn’t feel like a resolution at all, probably because it falls on a downbeat.) This statement is then repeated on the B diminished seventh chord moving to C major as the dominant of F-minor, then begins again on what is supposed to be E diminished seventh. It should be noted that, once all of these chords are combined, they form the 12 tones of the chromatic scale, which Schiff notes may be an omen of 12-tone music.

However, on the last occurrence, instead of completing the Edim7 chord, the bass and treble move upward to form a B-flat minor chord, which dashes the cycle and allows us to move forward. Here, the right hand begins to descend diatonically and the left hand begins to ascend chromatically through various keys using the first rhythm: this transitory part is usually played more gently compared to the gigantic chords of the first few bars, as per its piano dynamic. This descending phrase lasts 5 bars, which complement the first 5 bars of introduction proper.

The next 8 bars are considered as dominant preparation, primarily due to the dominant pedal in the alto voice and also because all of the upper progressions end in the dominant major. Again, the progression lasts only 2 bars, and then repeats itself one octave lower. The last 4 bars are used to transition from the Maestoso introduction to the Allegro con Brio ed Appassionato by repeating the dominant chord in the right hand and then using a measured-out trill in the extreme bass that should have the same note-value from one tempo to the other. (It should be noted that this is one of the most interesting ways of saying that the Maestoso should be half the speed of the Allegro before the notation for Metric Modulation was invented.)

However, even though this introduction is highly reminiscent of the introduction to the Sonata Pathetique, this introduction is different in one major way from the previous introductions: all of the thematic material that is used here is never seen again. The two themes of the introduction are almost independent of this introduction, with the only possible link being the second theme to the transition passage that Tovey dismisses as bunkum. Instead, it is the harmony of the introduction (and the dotted rhythm, but that’s probably accidental), with its very unique progression of diminished seventh chords, that is reused later on in the development.

Exposition

Theme Group 1

Note: Especially with Beethoven’s late themes, it becomes much more fruitful to think about it as a collection of motifs rather than a singular theme (or maybe a theme with a head and tail). This is because Beethoven plays around with different instances of these motifs, and combines them in interesting ways and extends them freely in order to generate the material of the piece. This is most apparent in the Hammerklavier sonata, where the 3rd part of the theme is extended for the better part of 6 bars before the second entrance in the fugue. But this sort of free extension happens with essentially all of the late sonatas that bother to be in sonata form. Thus, while there is only 1 theme in Theme Group 1, I will be dividing it up into motifs using letters: e.g. Motif 1a, 2b.

In any case, we begin with a very quick rise of four (or three, it depends if you’re OK with motifs overlapping) notes that go straight up from G to C: this is called the Schleifer by Tovey, and I will call it Motif 1a. This is mentioned in unison by both the left and and the right hand, forming a very powerful octave that adds to the starkness of the theme. After that, we have the rise yet again but now with an extension, the C-Eb-B motif that also comprises Theme 1 and that will be called Motif 1b (and which overlaps with Motif 1a, but the boundaries of motifs are very unclear and I just hear it that way). After a fermata on the dissonant B-natural, we get an entrance of the full Theme 1 in Bar 21 with motifs 1a, 1b, 1c, and 1d, with each motif overlapping with another, as can be seen in the following illustration:

Directly after that, just as we repeated 1a and 1b, we now repeat 1c and 1d with a little bit of hesitation and then extend the tail of the theme, 1c and 1d combined, but expressed in the rhythm of 1d for 5 bars. The easiest way to notice this is to pay careful attention to the accented notes of certain interpreters like Korstick, which will make the theme obvious: with other interpretations, you just have to pay special attention to the general contour of the theme and compare it to the memory of motifs 1c and 1d.

After that passage, we now state Theme 1 in its full form, with the first 3 motifs being harmonized by quarter notes in the alto right hand and by a counterpoint in the left hand and the last motif left alone. Similar to the previous statement of Theme 1, the tail of 1c and 1d are repeated again with hesitation, poco ritenente or slightly slowed down, with a slightly more extended harmony and a dominant pedal-point in both the left and and the right hand, before performing another free and virtuosic extension, this time on Motif 1c. (We should note that this is where we have the first instance of an octave counterpoint in the left hand: while most interpreters usually keep this to the role of accompaniment, this type of accompaniment-figure will become very important as we enter the transition.) Lastly, the music slows down a lot (to become quarter notes rather than the very fast 16th notes of the previous bar) and we get a cadence, a full close, by using an augmented (or slowed-down) form of motif 1d ornamented with a trill.

Transition

However, the close is suddenly broken by a forte , which means to play loud (in this case, suddenly): the left hands goes off into an 16th note extension of Motif 1d, while the right hand plays Motif 1b then a form of Motif 1c in augmentation but using the rhythm of Motif 1d. Here, it becomes really obvious that what looks on the surface to just be a free extension of 1d is actually 1c or 1c + 1d (it depends on interpretation) expressed in the rhythm of 1d, if you listen carefully to the sections that are delineated by the arpeggios. While the right hand plays this very fast figuration, the left hand plays an extension of 1c + 1d in full octaves, with 1d expressed in the rhythm of 1c. Thus, the two hands mirror each other, and at the same time play a counterpoint to each other. After this first statement, the left hand begins playing 1b in octaves, then passes that off to the right hand to play 1c + 1d (octave) thrice as the left hand takes the 1c + 1d (running notes) figuration. Again, in an even higher register, the right hand states 1b (in single notes), then passes off the 1c + 1d octaves to the left hand as it takes the 1c + 1d running notes. This very fast passage is considered a transition because it modulates a lot: from the beginning in C-minor, the first occurrence takes us up 2 steps to E-flat major, and the second occurrence takes us up 3 steps to A-flat major, and the third occurrence takes us all the way up 3 steps again to D-flat, which is really far away from C.

From this very distant place, Beethoven is able to take us back by using a very dramatic figure: he has the left hand go all the way back to the bass, play tremolo chords, then have the right hand crash all the way down from the very top of the keyboard to the bass then back up to resolve to E-flat, the dominant of A-flat major. This feels a lot like the explosion at the end of the Les Adieux sonata: after this, the music gets winded, and thus has to take a rest, and this rest forms the second theme group.

Theme Group 2

The second theme group, lasting only 2 bars, stands in stark contrast to the first, most of all because it is not stark: it is warm, which heavily contrasts with the dire and angered nature of the first theme and the transition and the glorious or humorous nature of the material after this theme. Schiff notes that it feels like an island in the roiling sea— I would add that it also feels like the eye of a hurricane, in a sense, albeit that metaphor falls apart once we see the second half of the exposition. According to Kumar, it also feels improvised: this is made clear by the second repetition of the second theme, where every other note has an ornament and where the tempo is altered very freely. Lastly, it is singular: it cannot be segmented into multiple meaningful motifs, as it is composed of just the syncopated rhythm repeated in different registers and harmonies. Thus, it heavily contrasts with everything that came before it. It reminds me heavily of the recitatives in the Tempest sonata: and much like those recitatives, this one is interrupted before it has the chance to stay long.

It is interrupted by a fortissimo falling arpeggio-flourish, outlining a D diminished seventh chord, which is exactly equal to an A-flat diminished seventh, spanning 3 full octaves. After a short scale, we now get an instance of Motif 1b three times with a new jumping-up figure that Tovey calls figure (e), and that I will call Motif 2e to represent the fact that it was introduced separately from the rest. This particular combination of motifs is very interesting: because it is a whole 4 bars long, it can equally be called a theme, and it can play over and over again because it resolves into itself. In any case, this odd combination of 1b + 2e moves over to the top voice of the right hand with alto voices and bass voices appearing that mimic the restatement of 1a + 1b + 1c in the domain of the first theme group. This part is unusual because it is solidly in the major key, but its unusually hollow structure gives us the sense that it could turn sour, go to the minor key, at any time.

Lastly, we reach the cadence. The motif 2e is transformed into a new scalar figure, with a trill being placed on the long note. This theme, regrettably, is associated with childhood and play where I come from, and thus it loses the grandeur of the previous statements of 1b [*3] + 2e in favor of a rather funny cadence. Even worse, the left hand descends so low that the tones are barely recognizable, making it feel more like a rumbling bass rather than a properly functioning accompaniment. This is probably not the effect that Beethoven intended— indeed, one can argue that this is a by-product of a rather malign process called reification, as per Keary (2019)— but it nevertheless stands in contrast to the rest of the sonata and emphasizes the nature of the disparate parts united together by their contrast. Finally, after a long cadenza-like flourish running upwards, we resolve on A-flat, then use motif 1a to go all the way back to the beginning.

Development

The development of this sonata is actually rather compressed: much as the sonatas of the classical form, this is only a small fraction of the size of the exposition. However, a lot happens in those few short moments. It can technically be divided into 3 parts, which will not be delineated by section headers given that they are fairly short.

First, we get one of the shortest modulations in probably the history of music: directly after the octave A that ends the exposition, we have a single octave G played piano, and then a forte D-major chord in its first inversion. Rosen notes that this is one of the most laconic and efficient modulations, given that it occurs in 2 notes: he says that they are “all that is necessary” (p. 244) to move us to G minor. In any case, a form of the first theme composed of 1a + 1b [*2] + 2e (the upward rush, then the dun-dun-dun, then the jumping up motif) is played in bare octaves (but still an octave apart, giving the illusion that the pianist is somehow playing 5 octaves) and in piano. However, at the point motif 2e is supposed to end in G, the first voice of what seems to be a new theme enters.

This new theme, in fact, takes 2 inspirations: it has the overall contour of a slowed-down or augmented motif 1b, and Rosen states that the intervals of the new theme are related to the dominant sevenths of the introduction. It is different from those most notably because it has a trill on the F-sharp, which directly leads to G: this will be the hint for anyone who attempts to parse the coming fugue that they are listening to the theme. For convenience, I will call this the Development theme, given that, much like the introductory material, it doesn’t occur anywhere else.

Now, we begin to have a double fugue on the development theme and on Theme 1, composed of motifs 1b + 1c (and sometimes) + 1d. This means that we will hear the two themes interlocking with each other and both being developed equally. The usual way that we listen to a fugue is we search for the theme or themes being stated in multiple voices, and try to see when it emergen and in what key it emerges. Fugal texture is when we hear consecutive mentions of the theme in different registers, or we hear overlapping mentions of the theme in different registers (this doesn't happen in this case, but it happens in other cases). In this case, we see that in bar 76, the theme enters in the alto voice in the key of G minor: in bar 78, the bass voice in C minor; and in bar 80, in the soprano voice in F minor. Similarly, in bar 76.5, motifs 1b + 1c appear in the bass voice in G minor; in bar 78.5, in the soprano voice in C minor; and in bar 80.5, in the alto voice in F minor.

Right afterwards, the fugue breaks apart: we hear the motifs in reverse order (1d + 1c) turned into fast but non-virtuosic 16th notes in the right hand, and we hear the trill that delineated the ending of the development theme in the left hand. After another repetition down 1 octave, the left hand begins to descend chromatically and the right hand’s sixteenth notes roil over (in a move that reminds me somewhat of Bar 32, but that’s rather baseless speculation and should be confirmed by a professional before being considered as fact) into a great tremolo that moves on the the next part.

This next part begins one bar before the double barline: the double barline states the change in key, not the change in part as with other uses of the double barline. We now have a fully-harmonized statement of Motif 1b rising up and up for five bars: combined with the tumultuous arpeggios in the left hand that rise in 16th notes with intervals as large as an octave and a third (amateurs beware), and this part can be very tricky for the performer and very dramatic for the listener. Nevertheless, there is still a method to the madness, so to speak: the lowest note of the left hand is always a G, whether it be the lowest G or the G that is still on the bass clef, something which Tovey states is a full-fledged “home dominant pedal” (p. 275). The final chords in the right hand also form the same progression as in the introduction: F-sharp diminished seventh in Bar 86, B diminished seventh in Bar 87, and E diminished seventh in Bar 88, and then again one octave up: F-sharp diminished seventh in Bar 89, and B diminished seventh in Bar 90.

However, that occurrence in Bar 90 is very special. If we look at the original statement of Motif 1b, it goes C-Eb-B: and here, yet again, we have C-Eb-B in the topmost voice. Given that we have now reached the “correct” statement of Motif 1b, the E diminished seventh chord is not stated again and instead the music uses the 1c + 1d (stated in the 16th note pattern of 1d) to go all the way back to the recapitulation.

Recapitulation

The recapitulation is very long, and much of it concerns already-exposed material. [Also, I’m running out of time to write.] Thus, instead of walking you through the minutiae of this section, I’ll just cover the changes that have been made to each of the theme groups and to the transition.

Theme Group 1

Unlike its counterpart some 4 pages ago, there is no repetition of Theme Group 1 in open octaves and then harmonized. Instead, in the sole occurrence of the full theme, the first statement of 1b + 1c + 1d lies in full octaves (with a middle note implied, so 5 voices) and the next 2 repetitions of 1b + 1c + 1d are both harmonized. What was once bar 32 has been altered, so instead of playing a tremolo pedal point in one hand the pedal is moved to the octave left and the right hand plays the octave extension of 1c + 1d. Those bars also contain a somewhat secretive modulation to the fourth scale degree of F minor: it is from this key that the new transition will begin.

Transition

This transition is very similar to the previous one, except it now begins on F minor: thus, the first entry occurs in F minor and 1c occurs thrice (AKA a rise of 3 notes); the second entry occurs in B-flat minor and 1c occurs twice (a rise of 2 notes); the third entry occurs in D-flat major. However, after the third entry, the counterpoint composed of the 1c + 1d octaves completely disappears: thus, the two voices return to homophony by both stating 1c + 1d running notes. (Note to the score-reader: the way I determined the key of the current run for this next part is to look at the first 16th note of the 4th grouping in the right hand.) Then, after rising through D natural, then E natural, then F-sharp, then G (this is the left-hand note), then C, we now reach the dramatic 2 bars. This is also changed: the left hand now does some almost-unnoticed harmonic progression, moving chromatically upwards, first from E-flat up to E natural then from F to F-sharp, until we reach the G.

Theme Group 2

The second theme group has a lot more alteration compared to the first theme group, albeit most of it is to the transitions between the main parts.

First, we have Theme 2 transposed directly to the tonic: this takes up the first 8 bars. However, instead of a compression like what we saw in the First Theme Group, we see an expansion, something like a second development of sorts, one that is very helpfully delineated between the 2 instances of the words “Tempo I”. The first 2 bars of this interpolation feel much more like a chordal extension of the harmony of Theme 2 proper, which allow its improvisational quality to continue on for a little bit longer: however, in reality, it is very well-measured, given that the bass falls from C minor to F minor, the subdominant, which appears many times throughout this work. The next 4 bars also extend somewhat naturally from the previous 2: for the first half, the bass states the melody with the right hand playing a rather sparse arpeggio figuration, then the right hand states the ornament and then continues on with the theme. However, at this point, Beethoven does something very interesting: instead of moving straight to the huge descending arpeggio cadenza of Bar 55, he takes a 4-bar diversion by using the right hand of the last bar to rise upward and upward, increasing the dynamic and the speed along the way. This causes a great feeling of unease, as what was once a rather innocent little figure grows to become a menace in its own right: this then climaxes at the falling arpeggio figure, which has been lengthened for another bar to allow us to get back to the home key of C minor from the rather distant space that had been reached.

Even this next part, which would fall under the scope of a false Theme 2.2, has changed (much as the second themes change in the Sonata Pathetique): no longer is it glorious, good-natured, or even funny as in the previous trills, but now rather dire and anxious, marching ever-onwards. The harmonies no longer suggest a calm sunset that would be rather touching and meaningful if it weren't for the stinted accompaniment: instead, both the accompaniment and the harmony now lend to the dour coloration of the music. The music here is really suggestive of a full orchestra, with full octavic treatment and constant register-switching: the accented bass cannot be loud enough to play, especially given that Beethoven marks it a full fortissimo, as loud as possible. This lasts all the way until the end of the Second Theme Group, with a long chromatic scale ending on the correct note of C.

Coda

The coda begins with these stark chords, all played fortissimo and sforzando. But then, the resolve of the music seems to weaken, and it gets tired after an admittedly very long series of outbursts: the chords begin to diminuendo and soften in attack. Soon, it reaches the piano dynamic, and a very picturesque scene is conjured (which is unusual, because the rest of this music has been non-programmatic, so it is odd that it ends in such a picturesque way): a bass ostinato rumbles on beneath, like ocean-waves diminished and corralled in an underground chamber as placid chords elucidate odd harmonies that try to escape but cannot, like small islands. This happens over and over again until finally, the harmony is able to escape and reach a cadence in the major: unlike the Picardy thirds of the baroque period, this change does not feel forced at all, in my opinion, but rather feels more like the only possible way out for the music, the truest (and also happiest) ending. There, the right hand repeats its major chord twice more, but I think does not insist on it: rather, it feels more relieved to have reached its final point of rest or escape, whereupon the music ends.

Conclusion

This is the last sonata that Beethoven wrote. After this, he wrote to his publisher that “the pianoforte is, after all, an unsatisfactory instrument” (Tovey, 280): his Ninth Symphony, Missa Solemnis, and five last string quartets are all written for ensembles that are greater in number, in size, and also somehow in expressive power than the piano. His only other piano works past this point would be the great Diabelli Variations, and the 6 Bagatelles, op. 126. The last movement, in particular, is so touching and meaningful that some have taken to calling it a “farewell to sonata form”.

While this work serves as the conclusion of this series of essays, not least because I don’t have any more time to write, I do not think that this should be a farewell to the analysis of Beethoven’s sonatas and of his works in general. There are many other works that I have failed to touch upon and that deserve touching upon: not only the greats like the Moonlight, Pastorale, Tempest, Waldstein, Appassionata, and Hammerklavier, but also the little sonatas like No. 1 Op 2/1 (the very first Beethoven wrote, see https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o5dL-65mKe0), No. 7 Op 10/3 (it has a very meaningful second movement, see https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CfiiRZvTVeU), No. 19 Op. 49/1 (Good for amateurs, lyrical but sad: see https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yqnwJmzXkac), No. 24 Op. 78 (Lyrical, like No. 19, but happy: see https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CfiiRZvTVeU ). All of these deserve at least a listen, or (if you know how to play the piano) a play-through. Just like the other works discussed, they also have strong forms and underlying structures that are also worth analyzing: this is where good books like Tovey’s A Companion to Beethoven’s Pianoforte Sonatas (which is very dense and has a lot of technical language) or Fischer’s Beethoven’s Pianoforte Sonatas (which is a lot more easygoing, but aimed at piano-players), and the videos liked above have program notes to help dissect the form as well. The ultimate goal of these essays is not to be the be-all and end-all work, but rather to serve as an introduction to the wide world of music theory and music analysis that Beethoven used to construct his works, and that give us deeper insight into those works as well. Thus, I hope that this will not serve as the conclusion but rather an introduction to Beethoven. Thank you for reading!
